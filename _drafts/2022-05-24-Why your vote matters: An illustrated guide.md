"My vote doesn't matter."

How many times have you heard this from people around you? Maybe you've even said it yourself.

In the end, we only get one vote each, what difference could it make?

I've had the same thought myself in the past, and I mostly voted out of a sense of civic duty. But I want to share a new way to think about the power of a single vote that convinced me to vote for every election from here on out, and might just convince you to do the same.

It's an idea I'm sure many of you are familiar with already:

Expected Value.

When we stop thinking of our vote as "just one vote," and instead think about our vote in terms of increasing the probability of a potential outcome, the value of our vote becomes far more clear.

Say you live in the small town of Sandchwichville, population 10,000 where there are two candidates for mayor on the city ballot. The city has a budget of \\$100,000 a year, which the mayor has full discretion to spend on the policies they support.
1. Candidate one supports a universal basic income of $1,000 / month for every person in the town
2. Candidate two supports a direct cash transfer of $10,000 a month, to only the poorest 10% of the city.



The 2022 midterm elections have already begun, starting with the primary elections. 35 of the 100 Senate seats are up for election, and all 435 House seats are up for election in 2022.

Voter turnout for midterm elections has historically been far below that of presidential elections. Even in the 2018 midterm election, which saw a huge spike up to 50% voter turnout compared to previous midterm years, pales in comparison ot the 66.7% voter turnout seen in the 2020 presidential elections. 

Several factors affect voter turnout, namely:
- Electoral competitiveness
- Election type
- Voting laws
- Demographics (age, race/ethnicity, gender, socio-economic status)

Elections that are more competitive (races seem closer) get more voter turnout. Presidential elections get more turnout than midterm elections. States that make it easier to vote (e.g. mail-in ballots) get more turnout. And older, wealthier, more educated, and white people turn out more elections.

Each of these factors could be explored far more deeply than I can hope to cover in one sitting, but today I'd like to address the number one reason I hear from people around me (younger, Asian, Californians) for why they don't vote:

"My vote doesn't matter."

This sentiment seems to capture several of the factors above. For instance, it could refer to the lack of electoral competitiveness in a state like California which leans heavily Democratic. It could refer to how because other youth tend to not vote, policies often favor the older and wealthier. It could refer to how minorities feel disempowered from changing the status quo when more than 75% of the country are of a different race or ethnicity.
